# Loss of urate oxidase activity in hominoids and its evolutionary implications

**Authors:** Masako Oda, Yoko Satta, Osamu Takenaka, Naoyuki Takahata  
**Journal:** Molecular Biology and Evolution, Volume 19, Issue 5, Pages 640–653  
**Year:** 2002  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.molbev.a004123  
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11961098/

## Abstract

The researchers examined the urate oxidase gene across various primate species. They confirmed that humans and great apes carry a CGA-to-TGA nonsense mutation in exon 2, while gibbons show independent mutations including a different nonsense mutation, base deletion in exon 3, or base insertion in exon 5. The functional urate oxidase gene in primates shows "strong selective constraint against nonsynonymous sites" despite its eventual inactivation. Substitutions in promoter regions and CAAT-boxes may explain reduced transcriptional activity. The authors propose a "two-step deterioration process" occurring first in the promoter and then in coding regions during primate evolution. They suggest the elevated uric acid in primate blood may reflect molecular coevolution with xanthine oxidoreductase, though they note uncertainty regarding whether urate oxidase loss relates to oxidative damage protection and extended lifespan.

## Relevance to Open Enzyme

This paper establishes the molecular genetics of uricase pseudogenization in great apes: a single CGA→TGA nonsense mutation in exon 2, with gibbons showing independently-evolved inactivation events. The two-step deterioration model (promoter first, then coding sequence) has implications for ancestral sequence reconstruction — there may be partial functional conservation deeper in the phylogeny than the coding sequence alone suggests. For Open Enzyme, this is foundational context explaining why humans require an exogenous uricase source and why gut-lumen delivery (circumventing systemic immunogenicity) is the favored approach over hepatic gene restoration.
