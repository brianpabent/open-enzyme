# Controlling heterologous gene expression in yeast cell factories on different carbon substrates and across the diauxic shift: a comparison of yeast promoter activities

**Authors:** Bingyin Peng, Thomas C. Williams, Matthew Henry, Lars K. Nielsen, Claudia E. Vickers  
**Journal:** Microbial Cell Factories, Volume 14, Article 91  
**Year:** 2015  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12934-015-0278-5  
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26112740/

## Abstract

The study examined how yeast promoters control gene expression under industrial fermentation conditions. Researchers characterized "constitutive" promoters (glycolytic, transcription elongation factor, and ribosomal) alongside inducible promoters across different carbon sources and fermentation phases. Results revealed that constitutive promoters decreased sharply during glucose depletion and the diauxic shift, while low-glucose-inducible promoters (HXT7, SSA1, ADH2) increased expression during ethanol consumption phases. The GAL1 promoter provided highest expression on galactose, and the copper-inducible CUP1 promoter enabled strong post-diauxic expression. These findings provide a foundation for optimizing metabolic engineering strategies in yeast cell factories across various bioprocess conditions and carbon sources.

## Relevance to Open Enzyme

This paper is the empirical reference for selecting promoters in S. cerevisiae/S. boulardii expression systems under fermentation-relevant conditions. The key finding — that glycolytic "constitutive" promoters (TEF1, PGK1, etc.) drop sharply during the diauxic shift — has direct implications for uricase expression stability during fed-batch or high-density fermentation. The low-glucose-inducible promoters (HXT7, SSA1, ADH2) that increase post-diauxically may actually be preferable for gut-expressed enzymes, since glucose concentrations in the lower GI are low. This promoter activity map is foundational context for the Wang et al. 2025 (S. boulardii) paper's constitutive promoter selection strategy and informs Open Enzyme's own promoter engineering choices.
