# BHB + cortisol interaction during fasting in a gout patient — a single-subject pharmacodynamic time-course.

1. **BHB + cortisol interaction during fasting in a gout patient — a single-subject pharmacodynamic time-course.** *Cost: ~$100. Time: 1 day (24h fast with serial measurements).* `[PHASE-A-MATCH: no]`

   - *What it tests:* Whether the cortisol elevation during prolonged fasting in a gout patient produces a clinically significant interaction with BHB's NLRP3 suppression and the fasting-associated UA spike. The three-axis interaction (BHB ↑, UA ↑ transiently, cortisol ↑) is uncharacterized in any gout-specific context.
   - *Protocol:* Brian (n=1). 24-hour fast from dinner-to-dinner. Serial fingerstick BHB (ketone meter) + UA (UASure) + salivary cortisol (commercial salivary cortisol ELISA kit, ~$50 for multi-strip pack) at t=0 (pre-fast), t=8h, t=16h, t=24h. Track any prodromal joint symptoms. Correlate cortisol peak timing with UA peak timing. If cortisol peaks before UA, the anti-inflammatory effect of cortisol may mask the UA spike — this would refine the "suspend fasting during flares" guidance by identifying a sub-population (high-cortisol-responders) where fasting is *less* flare-risky.
   - *Decides:* Whether the cortisol arm of the fasting response needs to be incorporated into the BHB prophylactic-vs-acute-flare clinical integration framework. If cortisol and UA peaks are temporally separated (cortisol early, UA late), the fasting-flare risk profile is more nuanced than the current binary contraindication suggests.

> **Pass 3 review — Partial.** `[OVERLAP: EXTENSION]` `[GAP: science-gap]` The experiment is cheap and biologically interesting, but its decision logic overinterprets timing correlations: if salivary cortisol peaks before a UASure-measured UA peak during a 24-hour fast, that does not establish that fasting is less flare-risky in "high-cortisol responders." At most it would generate a within-subject time-course for BHB, UA, and cortisol under standardized fasting conditions; any claim about masked flare risk or steroid-rebound biology needs a separate literature anchor and repeat observations.
