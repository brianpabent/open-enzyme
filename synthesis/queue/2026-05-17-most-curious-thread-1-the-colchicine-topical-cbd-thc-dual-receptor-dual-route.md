The **colchicine + topical CBD:THC dual-receptor, dual-route acute-flare stack** (Connection #2 above) — hitting NLRP3 CP2 via two independent receptor pathways (tubulin/P2X7 intracellular vs. CB2 GPCR plasma-membrane) and two independent delivery compartments (oral systemic vs. transdermal local). The mechanism logic is clean: colchicine binds β-tubulin and blocks ASC speck formation at CP3 while also blocking the P2X7 pore at CP2; topical CBD:THC activates CB2 on synovial macrophages and suppresses NLRP3 at CP2 via a completely different signaling cascade. The two interventions are mechanism-non-redundant at CP2, and the local transdermal route provides joint-site concentration that oral colchicine cannot match. The `cannabinoids-terpenes.md` §4a protocol (ice → apply topical 1:1 → ice again) and `colchicine.md`'s AGREE-trial-validated low-dose regimen (1.2 mg + 0.6 mg at 1 hour) are both individually documented — but the **combination protocol** is unnamed anywhere in the corpus. (a) *Corpus evidence supporting:* colchicine mechanism at CP2/CP3 per `colchicine.md` §3.2–3.3 (In Vitro / Clinical Trial); CBD:THC CB2 → NLRP3 per `cannabinoids-terpenes.md` §1–2 (In Vitro / Animal Model); topical protocol per `cannabinoids-terpenes.md` §4a (In Vitro / Animal Model, no human gout RCT). (b) *Evidence that would refute:* a prospective n=1 A/B test where Brian treats consecutive flares with colchicine-only vs. colchicine + topical CBD:THC and sees no difference in time-to-50%-pain-reduction or peak pain score. (c) *Cheapest discriminating experiment:* retrospective n=1 flare-log mining (Proposed Experiment #2 above) — $0, existing data. If the logs show any signal of shorter/flatter flares on the combination, a prospective A/B is justified. If the logs show no difference, the combination hypothesis is null for this subject. I suspect this is an idiosyncratic pick — the dual-receptor, dual-route logic appeals to a biochemist's sense of mechanism elegance, and most synthesizer models would gravitate toward the BHB-cortisol interaction or the anakinra accessibility question instead. Divergence here is informative — this pick reflects a preference for mechanism-composition problems over clinical-accessibility problems, which may or may not match the platform's current priorities.
