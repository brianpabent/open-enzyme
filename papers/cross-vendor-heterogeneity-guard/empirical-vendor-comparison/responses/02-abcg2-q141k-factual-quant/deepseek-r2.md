1. **Fraction of total uric acid excretion mediated by ABCG2**  
   ABCG2 is the primary transporter for extra‑renal (intestinal) urate excretion. In healthy humans, extra‑renal elimination accounts for approximately **35% (roughly one‑third)** of total urate excretion. Therefore, ABCG2 mediates about one‑third of total uric acid excretion.  
   *Source:* Ichida et al. *Nat Commun* 2012;3:764. (Stable‑isotope study showing extra‑renal urate excretion = 35.8 ± 12.3% of total; ABCG2 dysfunction significantly reduces this fraction.)

2. **Functional consequence of Q141K on transporter activity**  
   The Q141K variant reduces urate transport by approximately **50%** relative to wild‑type. In *Xenopus* oocyte expression assays, Q141K decreased urate uptake by **53%** (P < 0.001).  
   *Source:* Woodward et al. *Proc Natl Acad Sci USA* 2009;106:10338‑10342.

3. **Minor‑allele frequency (MAF) of Q141K (rs2231142)**  
   - East Asian populations: **~29%** (Japanese MAF = 0.29)  
   - European populations: **~11%** (European American MAF = 0.11)  
   *Source:* Woodward et al. 2009 (genotyping of Japanese and European‑ancestry cohorts). Similar frequencies are reported in gnomAD.

4. **Odds ratio for gout associated with Q141K**  
   In a Caucasian case‑control study, **heterozygous** carriers of Q141K had an odds ratio for gout of **1.68** (95% CI 1.18–2.39) compared with wild‑type homozygotes.  
   *Source:* Woodward et al. *Proc Natl Acad Sci USA* 2009.

5. **Dominant site of ABCG2‑mediated uric acid excretion: gut vs. kidney**  
   The **gut** is the dominant site. Evidence:  
   - In humans, ABCG2 dysfunction markedly reduces extra‑renal (intestinal) urate excretion, while renal urate excretion remains unchanged (Ichida et al. 2012).  
   - In *Abcg2*‑knockout mice, intestinal urate excretion is severely impaired, renal excretion is normal, and the animals develop hyperuricemia (Ichida et al. 2012).  
   Thus, ABCG2‑dependent urate elimination occurs primarily via the intestine.
