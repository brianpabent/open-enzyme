1. **Fraction of total uric acid excretion mediated by ABCG2**  
   ABCG2 accounts for approximately **30 % (roughly one‑third)** of total urate excretion in healthy humans. This estimate comes from a study that compared renal and extra‑renal urate clearance in individuals carrying loss‑of‑function ABCG2 variants and was supported by mouse models.  
   *Source:* Ichida K, Matsuo H, Takada T, et al. Decreased extra‑renal urate excretion is a common cause of hyperuricemia. *Nat Commun*. 2012;3:764. (The authors state: “We estimate that ABCG2‑mediated urate excretion accounts for approximately 30% of total urate excretion.”)

2. **Functional consequence of Q141K on transporter activity**  
   The Q141K variant reduces urate transport activity by **~53 %** relative to wild‑type ABCG2 (i.e., ~47 % residual activity). This was determined in Xenopus oocyte and HEK293 cell‑based transport assays.  
   *Source:* Woodward OM, Köttgen A, Coresh J, et al. Identification of a urate transporter, ABCG2, with a common functional polymorphism causing gout. *Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A*. 2009;106(25):10338‑10342. (Reported as a 53 % reduction in urate transport rate for the Q141K variant.)

3. **Minor‑allele frequency (MAF) of Q141K (rs2231142)**  
   - **East Asian populations:** MAF ≈ **0.30** (range ~0.29–0.30)  
   - **European populations:** MAF ≈ **0.09** (range ~0.09–0.10)

4. **Odds ratio for gout associated with Q141K**  
   In a New Zealand case‑control study, the heterozygous genotype (GA) was associated with an **odds ratio of 1.74 (95 % CI 1.41–2.14)** for gout compared with the wild‑type (GG) genotype. The homozygous variant genotype (AA) gave an OR of 2.94 (1.87–4.63).  
   *Source:* Woodward OM, et al. *Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A*. 2009 (Table 2).

5. **Dominant site of ABCG2‑mediated uric acid excretion: gut vs. kidney**  
   The **gut is the dominant site**. ABCG2 is highly expressed on the apical membrane of intestinal epithelial cells and excretes urate into the intestinal lumen. Evidence:  
   - Ichida et al. (2012) showed that humans with ABCG2 dysfunction have markedly reduced extra‑renal (intestinal) urate excretion, and *Abcg2*‑knockout mice develop hyperuricemia with normal renal urate handling but diminished intestinal urate output.  
   - Direct measurement of urate in intestinal fluid after purine loading confirmed ABCG2‑dependent secretion (Hosomi et al. 2012, *Clin Chim Acta*).  
   While ABCG2 is also present in the kidney proximal tubule, its contribution to renal urate excretion is minor compared with its intestinal role; the kidney handles the majority of total urate excretion, but ABCG2‑mediated excretion in the gut is the primary pathway that, when impaired, leads to hyperuricemia.
