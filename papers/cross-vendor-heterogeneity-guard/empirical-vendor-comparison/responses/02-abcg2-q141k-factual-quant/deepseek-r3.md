1. **Fraction of total uric acid excretion mediated by ABCG2**  
   Approximately **30% (one‑third)** of total uric acid excretion in healthy humans is mediated by ABCG2, primarily through intestinal secretion. This estimate derives from human clearance studies showing that extra‑renal excretion accounts for ~30% of total urate elimination, combined with the finding that ABCG2 is the major transporter responsible for that route. (Ichida et al., 2012, *Nature Communications* 3:764; evidence level: moderate, based on human physiology and Abcg2‑knockout mouse data.)

2. **Functional consequence of Q141K on transporter activity**  
   The Q141K variant reduces urate transport activity by **~50–55%** relative to wild‑type ABCG2 in vitro. In Xenopus oocyte expression assays, Q141K showed a 53% reduction in urate transport rate (Woodward et al., 2009, *PNAS* 106:10338–10342; evidence level: strong, direct functional measurement).

3. **Minor‑allele frequency (MAF) of Q141K (rs2231142)**  
   - (a) East Asian populations: **~0.29 (29%)**  
   - (b) European (non‑Finnish) populations: **~0.11 (11%)**  
   (gnomAD v2.1.1; Karczewski et al., 2020, *Nature* 581:434–443; evidence level: strong, large population sequencing data.)

4. **Published odds ratio for gout associated with Q141K**  
   In a case‑control study of White individuals, the **homozygous Q141K genotype (AA)** was associated with an odds ratio of **2.80 (95% CI 1.66–4.74)** for gout compared to the wild‑type (CC) genotype (Woodward et al., 2009, *PNAS* 106:10338–10342; evidence level: moderate, single case‑control study).

5. **Dominant site of ABCG2‑mediated uric acid excretion**  
   **The gut is the dominant site.** ABCG2 is highly expressed on the apical membrane of small intestinal epithelial cells and mediates secretion of urate into the intestinal lumen. In Abcg2‑knockout mice, intestinal urate excretion is drastically reduced while renal excretion increases compensatorily. In humans, the Q141K variant primarily impairs extra‑renal (gut) urate excretion, leading to hyperuricemia, with a smaller effect on renal fractional excretion. Although ABCG2 is also present in the kidney proximal tubule and contributes to renal urate secretion, quantitative data show that the intestinal route accounts for the majority of ABCG2‑dependent urate elimination (Ichida et al., 2012, *Nature Communications* 3:764; evidence level: strong, from animal models and human genotype–phenotype studies).
