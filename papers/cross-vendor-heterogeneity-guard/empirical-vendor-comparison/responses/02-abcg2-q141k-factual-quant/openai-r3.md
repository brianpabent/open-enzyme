### ABCG2 c.421C>A / p.Q141K / rs2231142 and urate handling

| Item | Answer |
|---|---|
| **1. Fraction of total uric-acid excretion mediated by ABCG2 in healthy humans** | **Best practical estimate: ~30% of total urate disposal, but this is inferential rather than a direct human ABCG2-flux measurement.** The ~30% corresponds to the **extra-renal/gut urate-excretion component**, which human genetic/physiologic data attribute largely to ABCG2. Ichida et al. showed that ABCG2 dysfunction selectively reduces **extra-renal urate excretion** and produces a compensatory “renal overload” phenotype, supporting ABCG2 as the major mediator of this component. Evidence level: **moderate human physiologic-genetic inference**, not direct transporter-specific isotope flux. Source: Ichida et al., *Nat Commun* 2012. |
| **2. Functional consequence of Q141K** | Q141K is a **partial loss-of-function** allele. In cell/vesicle transport assays, urate transport is reduced by about **50% relative to wild-type**; many genetic-physiology papers model the Q141K allele as **~50% activity**. The mechanism includes reduced ABCG2 protein abundance/maturation/trafficking rather than a pure catalytic defect. Sources: Woodward et al., *PNAS* 2009; Matsuo et al., *Sci Transl Med* 2009. |
| **3a. Minor-allele frequency, East Asian populations** | Approximately **0.25–0.35** for the 421A / 141K allele; a useful round number is **~0.30**. Frequencies vary among Japanese, Han Chinese, Korean, etc. Source: 1000 Genomes / gnomAD population data. |
| **3b. Minor-allele frequency, European populations** | Approximately **0.09–0.12**; a useful round number is **~0.10**. Source: 1000 Genomes / gnomAD population data. |
| **4. Example published gout/hyperuricemia risk estimate** | In a Japanese case-control study, Matsuo et al. reported that individuals with **mild ABCG2 dysfunction**, essentially including **heterozygous Q141K without the null Q126X allele** and modeled as ~75% ABCG2 function, had increased gout risk: **OR ≈ 2.35** versus full-function genotypes. Severe dysfunction, including lower-function genotypes such as Q141K homozygosity and/or Q126X, had still higher risk. Source: Matsuo et al., *Sci Transl Med* 2009. |
| **5. Dominant site: gut or kidney?** | **Gut/intestine is the dominant site of ABCG2-mediated urate excretion.** ABCG2 is present in both renal proximal tubule apical membrane and intestinal epithelium, but the key evidence points to intestinal ABCG2 as the major physiologic route: (i) **Abcg2-null mice** have markedly reduced intestinal urate secretion, while urinary urate excretion is not reduced and can rise secondarily; (ii) in humans, ABCG2 loss-of-function produces **decreased extra-renal urate excretion** with compensatory renal urate overload, rather than a primary renal underexcretion phenotype. Sources: Hosomi et al., *PLoS One* 2012; Ichida et al., *Nat Commun* 2012. |
