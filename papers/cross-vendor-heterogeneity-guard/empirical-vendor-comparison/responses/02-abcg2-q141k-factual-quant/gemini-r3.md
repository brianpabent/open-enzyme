### ABCG2 and the c.421C>A (Q141K) Polymorphism in Uric Acid Homeostasis

The ATP-binding cassette sub-family G member 2 (ABCG2), also known as breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP), is a high-capacity ATP-dependent efflux transporter. While first identified for its role in multidrug resistance, it is now established as a critical regulator of systemic uric acid levels. The common non-synonymous polymorphism c.421C>A (p.Gln141Lys or Q141K, rs2231142) significantly impairs its function.

**1. Fraction of Total Uric Acid Excretion Mediated by ABCG2**

In healthy humans, uric acid excretion is partitioned between the renal (~70%) and extra-renal (primarily intestinal, ~30%) routes. ABCG2 is the principal, physiologically-relevant transporter mediating the extra-renal (intestinal) secretion of urate. Therefore, ABCG2 is responsible for a substantial portion of this extra-renal pathway, which corresponds to **up to approximately one-third of total systemic uric acid excretion**.

*   **Evidence Level:** High. This was established through a combination of human genetics, *in vitro* transport assays, and studies using *Abcg2*-knockout mice, which exhibit severe hyperuricemia due to a profound defect in intestinal urate excretion.
*   **Primary Source:** Ichida, K. et al. Decreased extra-renal urate excretion is a common cause of hyperuricemia. *Nat. Commun.* **3**, 764 (2012). [DOI: 10.1038/ncomms1756](https://doi.org/10.1038/ncomms1756)

**2. Functional Consequence of Q141K on Transporter Activity**

The Q141K polymorphism results in a significant reduction in transporter function. *In vitro* studies using [¹⁴C]-urate transport assays in *Xenopus laevis* oocytes demonstrated that the Q141K variant exhibits a **~53% reduction in urate transport activity** compared to the wild-type (Q141) protein. This is believed to be caused by reduced cell-surface expression and potentially altered ATP hydrolysis.

*   **Evidence Level:** High (based on multiple concordant *in vitro* studies).
*   **Primary Source:** Woodward, O. M. et al. Identification of a common variant of ABCG2 that causes gout. *Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. U.S.A.* **106**, 10338–10342 (2009). [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.0901249106](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0901249106)

**3. Minor Allele Frequency (MAF) of Q141K (rs2231142)**

The 'A' allele (encoding Lys141) shows marked variation in frequency across global populations.

*   **(a) East Asian populations:** The MAF is high, approximately **30-35%**.
*   **(b) European populations (Non-Finnish):** The MAF is substantially lower, approximately **10-12%**.

*   **Evidence Level:** High (based on large-scale population sequencing databases).
*   **Primary Source:** Data aggregated in the Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD). For rs2231142, gnomAD v3.1.2 reports an allele frequency of 0.339 for East Asians and 0.113 for European (Non-Finnish) populations. (Accessed October 2023).

**4. Odds Ratio for Hyperuricemia/Gout Associated with Q141K**

The 'A' allele of rs2231142 is strongly associated with an increased risk of hyperuricemia and gout. In a landmark genome-wide association study (GWAS), the variant was associated with hyperuricemia with the following risk estimate:

*   **Odds Ratio (OR): 1.67** (95% Confidence Interval: 1.48–1.88; *P* = 7.9 x 10⁻¹⁷) for hyperuricemia **per copy of the A-allele (risk allele)**.

*   **Evidence Level:** High (GWAS meta-analysis). This per-allele model implies a dose-dependent effect, where risk is higher for homozygous (A/A) individuals than for heterozygous (C/A) individuals.
*   **Primary Source:** Dehghan, A. et al. Association of three genetic loci with uric acid concentration and risk of gout: a genome-wide association study. *Lancet* **372**, 1953–1961 (2008). [DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(08)61343-4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(08)61343-4)

**5. Dominant Site of ABCG2-Mediated Uric Acid Excretion: Gut vs. Kidney**

The **gut** is the dominant site where ABCG2 dysfunction exerts its primary effect on systemic uric acid levels.

*   **Evidence:**
    1.  **Expression and Physiology:** While ABCG2 is expressed on the apical membrane of both renal proximal tubule cells and intestinal epithelia, its role in the kidney is one of several redundant secretory and reabsorptive pathways (involving URAT1, OAT1, OAT3, etc.). In contrast, it is the predominant transporter responsible for intestinal urate secretion.
    2.  **Animal Models:** *Abcg2*-knockout mice exhibit a >50% reduction in extra-renal (intestinal) urate clearance but maintain or even compensatorily increase renal urate excretion. This demonstrates that the hyperuricemia in these animals is driven by the loss of the intestinal excretion pathway, not a primary renal defect.
    3.  **Human Genetics:** The large effect size of the Q141K variant on gout risk is most consistent with the disruption of a rate-limiting step. Given the redundancy in the renal pathway, it is more plausible that this effect is driven by the impairment of the non-redundant, ABCG2-dominant intestinal pathway.

*   **Primary Source for Animal Model Evidence:** Ichida, K. et al. *Nat. Commun.* **3**, 764 (2012).
