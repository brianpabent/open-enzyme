### ABCG2 c.421C>A / p.Q141K (rs2231142) and urate handling

| Item | Answer |
|---|---|
| **1. Fraction of total uric-acid excretion mediated by ABCG2 in healthy humans** | **Approx. ~30% (about one-third), but this is an inferred—not directly flux-measured—ABCG2-specific number in healthy humans.** The one-third figure comes from human urate balance studies showing that **extrarenal/intestinal elimination accounts for roughly one-third of total urate disposal**; ABCG2 is the major identified apical intestinal urate efflux transporter. Primary human physiology: Sørensen showed substantial intestinal urate elimination, classically ~1/3 of total urate disposal (Sørensen, *Arthritis Rheum.*, 1965). Human genetic/physiologic assignment of the extrarenal component to ABCG2: Ichida et al. found ABCG2 dysfunction associated with **decreased extrarenal urate excretion** and compensatory renal overload (Ichida et al., *Nat Commun.*, 2012). |
| **2. Functional consequence of Q141K** | **Loss-of-function / reduced urate transport.** In vitro, Q141K ABCG2 shows approximately **~50% residual urate transport activity** relative to wild type—i.e. about a **45–50% reduction**. Matsuo et al. reported Q141K as having about **53% of wild-type urate transport activity** and used this in a function-based genetic model (Matsuo et al., *Sci Transl Med.*, 2009). Mechanistically, Q141K reduces mature ABCG2 expression/trafficking and transporter activity. |
| **3a. Minor-allele frequency: East Asian populations** | **~0.25–0.35; commonly ~0.29–0.31 for the A/K141 allele.** 1000 Genomes Phase 3 East Asian frequency is approximately **0.29** for rs2231142-A. |
| **3b. Minor-allele frequency: European populations** | **~0.09–0.12; commonly ~0.10 for the A/K141 allele.** 1000 Genomes Phase 3 European frequency is approximately **0.09–0.10**. |
| **4. Example risk estimate for gout/hyperuricemia** | In the Japanese function-based case-control analysis of Matsuo et al., ABCG2 dysfunction categories corresponding approximately to Q141K dosage showed increased gout risk. **Moderate ABCG2 dysfunction**, which includes **Q141K homozygosity in the absence of a null allele** and corresponds to ~50% total ABCG2 function, had an odds ratio of about **OR ~3.0** for gout; **mild dysfunction**, roughly corresponding to **Q141K heterozygosity** with otherwise normal ABCG2, had **OR ~2.3** (Matsuo et al., *Sci Transl Med.*, 2009). These are functional-category ORs rather than a pure single-SNP genotype-only OR. |
| **5. Dominant site: gut vs kidney** | **Gut / extrarenal intestine is the dominant site of ABCG2-mediated urate excretion.** Although ABCG2 is expressed in kidney proximal tubule as well as intestine, the strongest in-vivo evidence points to intestinal efflux as the clinically dominant ABCG2-dependent route. Human evidence: ABCG2 loss-of-function is associated with **reduced extrarenal urate excretion** and a “renal overload” pattern—urinary urate excretion can rise secondarily because intestinal disposal is impaired (Ichida et al., *Nat Commun.*, 2012). Animal evidence: Abcg2/Bcrp knockout or inhibition markedly reduces intestinal urate secretion and raises plasma urate, supporting intestinal ABCG2 as a major urate-elimination pathway (Hosomi et al., *PLoS ONE*, 2012). |
