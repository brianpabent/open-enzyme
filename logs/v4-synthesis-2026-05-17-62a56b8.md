### Finding 1: The acute-flare abort stack has a hidden CP5a × CP6b temporal-composition logic that the wiki doesn't name

The `gout-action-guide.md` now has the acute-flare-abort comparator table (propagated from `gout-clinical-pipeline.md §6a`) listing anakinra SC, canakinumab SC, prednisone, colchicine+NSAID, and future inhaled mRNA-IL-1RA. Separately, `chassis-pending-interventions.md §M1` names the PDB × disulfiram multi-chassis stack for urate-disposal upstream + CP6b pyroptotic-exit blockade. And `disulfiram.md` positions disulfiram as a chronic prophylactic GSDMD inhibitor.

What nobody has named yet: **during an acute flare, anakinra SC (CP5a IL-1R1 blockade) and disulfiram (CP6b GSDMD pore blockade) hit complementary nodes of the same inflammatory cascade with different kinetics.** Anakinra is fast-onset (hours, SC), short half-life (~4-6 hours), and blocks the receptor — it stops IL-1β *signaling* on target cells. Disulfiram's GSDMD Cys191 covalent modification blocks the *exit route* — it prevents IL-1β that's already been cleaved by caspase-1 from being released through the pyroptotic pore. The two mechanisms are temporally complementary: anakinra blocks the signal that's already extracellular; disulfiram blocks the amplification from cells that are still pyroptosing.

The practical composition: a patient in an active flare could take **anakinra 100 mg SC daily × 3 days (abort the flare) + a single loading dose of disulfiram at the sub-AUD 100 mg (per comp-027's YELLOW-leaning-GREEN window) to cap the GSDMD-mediated amplification tail.** This is a two-drug, two-mechanism acute-flare protocol that hits CP5a (receptor) and CP6b (pore) simultaneously, with anakinra doing the heavy lifting on onset and disulfiram preventing the pyroptotic amplification that extends flare duration.

**Why this isn't just "stack two drugs":** the temporal logic matters. Anakinra's 4-6 hour half-life means it clears before the flare fully resolves; disulfiram's covalent GSDMD modification persists for the life of the modified protein (hours to ~1 day), covering the tail. The combination converts anakinra's short-window receptor blockade into a longer effective coverage by shutting down the IL-1β supply side (GSDMD pores) after the receptor side is already blocked. This is a *sequential* rather than *parallel* stack — the order matters.

**Evidence caveat:** no published study has tested anakinra + disulfiram co-administration in any context. The mechanism logic is sound (different chokepoints, different molecular targets, no known PK interaction — disulfiram is a CYP2E1 inhibitor, anakinra is a recombinant protein not metabolized by CYPs), but this is Mechanistic Extrapolation composed from Clinical Trial (anakinra in gout) + In Vitro (disulfiram GSDMD mechanism) + In Silico (comp-027 dose window). A single n=1 test during a future flare would be the cheapest empirical probe.

### Finding 2: The PDB-selenium axis and the acute-flare BHB contraindication share a renal-transporter competition mechanism that creates a hidden stacking conflict

`bhb-ketones.md` (propagated in this sweep) now explicitly states: "Do NOT use during an active flare — the transient UA spike from ketone-urate competition compounds the flare." The mechanism is ketone bodies competing with urate for renal MCT/URAT1 reabsorption. `purine-degrading-bacteria.md` documents that PDB's DOPDH enzyme is selenium-dependent and runs 27× faster with selenium than without, and that selenium deficiency could phenocopy PDB functional depletion.

The connection nobody has named: **during an acute flare, a patient who suspends ketosis (per the BHB contraindication) AND has marginal selenium status is losing two independent urate-disposal mechanisms simultaneously.** The ketosis suspension removes the BHB-driven NLRP3 suppression (CP1-CP3); the marginal selenium status means gut PDB are already operating at a fraction of their capacity. The net effect during a flare is a double-hit: less inflammasome suppression (from suspended BHB) AND less gut urate disposal (from selenium-limited PDB). Both hits converge on higher free urate available for MSU crystallization — exactly the wrong direction during a flare.

This is not a drug-drug interaction — it's a **dietary-status × metabolic-state interaction** that the wiki's per-page treatment misses because BHB and PDB live on different pages and the selenium angle is buried in PDB's "Cofactor Requirements" section. The practical implication: a gout patient entering a flare should not only suspend ketosis but also consider a one-time selenium-status check (serum selenium, ~$40-80) to rule out the double-hit. If selenium is low-normal, a short course of supplementation (100-200 µg/day selenomethionine) during the flare-recovery window could support PDB-mediated gut urate disposal while BHB is off the table.

**Evidence caveat:** no interventional study has tested selenium supplementation during acute gout flares. The PDB-selenium mechanism is Mechanistic Extrapolation from the DOPDH biochemistry (In Vitro) and the FARMM antibiotic-depletion study (Human Observational). The BHB-flare contraindication is Mechanistic Extrapolation from ketone-urate renal competition (well-established physiology). The composition is a two-step Mechanistic Extrapolation.

### Finding 6: The BHB active-flare contraindication (newly propagated) and the gout-action-guide's acute-flare section create a tension with the NLRP3 exploit map's "ketosis paradox resolved" framing that the wiki doesn't address

`bhb-ketones.md` now explicitly states: "Do NOT use BHB / exogenous ketones during a flare — ketone bodies and urate compete for renal MCT/URAT1 reabsorption; transient ketotic UA rise of 5–10% is documented and can compound the flare." `gout-action-guide.md` "Active flare" section says: "Avoid BHB / exogenous ketones during a flare." Both pages agree.

But `nlrp3-exploit-map.md` §"The Uric Acid Paradox: Resolved" has carried the framing since April 2026: "With the engineered koji handling uric acid levels, ketosis becomes pure upside — all NLRP3 suppression, no uric acid penalty." And `bhb-ketones.md` itself says: "The paradox resolves if you combine BHB with uricase or other uric acid-lowering therapy."

The tension: **the "paradox resolved" framing assumes the uricase is already active and controlling UA levels.** During an active flare, the engineered koji is NOT yet deployed (Phase 0), and even if it were, the urate mobilization from tophi dissolution during a flare could temporarily overwhelm the gut-lumen sink. The "paradox resolved" story is a **steady-state intercritical claim**, not an **acute-flare claim**. But the wiki doesn't distinguish these two temporal contexts — it says "BHB is pure upside with uricase" as a general statement, and separately says "don't use BHB during a flare" as a specific contraindication, without reconciling the two.

The resolution: **the "ketosis paradox resolved" framing needs a temporal qualifier.** During intercritical periods (no active flare, stable UA), BHB + uricase (or allopurinol-controlled UA) is indeed pure upside — NLRP3 suppression without a clinically meaningful UA penalty. During an active flare or the immediate post-flare recovery window (1-2 weeks), the transient UA spike from ketosis can compound the flare, and BHB should be suspended regardless of uricase status. Once the flare resolves and UA restabilizes, BHB can resume.

This is not a contradiction between pages — it's a missing temporal-context qualifier on a claim that's currently stated as a general principle.

## Riskiest Assumption

The single load-bearing belief in the current platform thesis that is least supported by the corpus is the **chaperone-orthogonal stacking framework's α-coefficient transferability from non-koji in vitro folding kinetics to in vivo *A. oryzae* ER-assisted folding.** The framework (`chaperone-orthogonal-stacking.md`) derives per-architecture PDI residence time coefficients (α = 0.3–0.6 for CCP/SCR sushi fold, α = 1.0 reference for Ig-like, α = 1.5–2.5 for transferrin-lobe) from mammalian and yeast in vitro studies — Notari 2023's lactoferrin oxidative folding kinetics were measured from a fully denatured state; Schmidt 2010's CCP-domain rigidity is a structural inference, not a kinetic measurement. The framework's own §8 item 6 explicitly names this as the calibration uncertainty: "in vivo ER-assisted co-translational folding with PDI acting on the nascent chain may be substantially faster, collapsing α toward ~1.0." The §3.5.4 calibration set (§1.9 lactoferrin + §1.25 DAF SCR1-4 under harmonized NSlD-ΔP10 / solid-state conditions) is designed to resolve this, but **until that wet-lab data lands, every architecture decision downstream of the framework — DAF SCR1-4 separate-strain routing, triple-cassette feasibility, the single-strain endgame thesis — rests on coefficients that have never been measured in the actual host organism under the actual fermentation format.** The framework is internally consistent and biochemically grounded, but its predictive power for *A. oryzae* solid-state koji is an extrapolation, not a measurement.
