# comp-004: Supplement ABCG2 Antagonism — Pharmacological Risk Assessment

**Question:** Do common dietary polyphenol supplements (quercetin, EGCG, curcumin) reach gut-lumen concentrations sufficient to inhibit ABCG2-mediated urate efflux from blood into the gut?

**Verdict: VERY HIGH risk for quercetin and curcumin.** Both reach 6.8–8.3× their ABCG2 IC50 in the gut lumen at standard supplement doses, predicting ~87–89% inhibition of ABCG2 transport. EGCG acts via ABCG2 expression downregulation, not acute transport inhibition — a different experimental design is needed.

**Informs:** [`validation-experiments.md` §1.14](../../wiki/validation-experiments.md) — supplement arms of the Caco-2 urate flux assay

## Key results

| Compound | Dose (mg) | Gut conc (µM) | IC50 (nM) | Risk ratio | Pred. inhibition | Risk level |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quercetin | 500 | 49.6 | 7,250 | 6.8× | 87% | **VERY_HIGH** |
| EGCG | 400 | 1,090 | N/A | N/A | N/A | **NOT_APPLICABLE** |
| Curcumin | 1,000 | 13.6 | 1,630 | 8.3× | 89% | **VERY_HIGH** |

All three compounds are solubility-limited in the gut lumen. Curcumin's paradox: <1% bioavailability concentrates >99% of the oral dose in the gut lumen, and its low ABCG2 IC50 (1,630 nM) makes it the highest-risk compound despite gut lumen concentration lower than quercetin.
